## Mentor Interview — Industry Professional, AI Security Engineer

- People I have mentored have different backgrounds, both Japan and Myanmar.
- How many I have mentored — 13 people in a year who are in Japan, mainly IT professionals (Japan context).
- How do the mentees approach her — Ma Thandar has a career service platform and accepts mentees through it for that platform. It is focused on career. Mentees include people who are mid-career and people who are graduating. 2 people failed and 11 people succeeded.
- Why fail — mentor and mentee misalignment; one changed direction and went for further study; another chose her own personal goal. We ask them not to extend the contract.
- Currently — 2 people are under a mentoring program, 9 people already got jobs. Our only goal is to get a job.
- The timeframe for mentoring — 2 months for personal commitment, but our proposal is 4 or 6 months. We requested at least a 2 month commitment.
- Communication issues — we meet twice per month, the rest is asynchronous communication. There isn't much reply in asynchronous communication if we give them tasks. One person has to always follow up.
- Success story — a person approached me to take the service, we had our initial discussion and I decided I could mentor her. Challenges — visa issues, similar to contract jobs — unstable jobs. We helped them with CV preparation, applying for jobs, interview practice, and even the onboarding process. Now she got an offer from a big company in Japan.
- As a mentor — I look at the background of the mentee. Even professional agencies look at the matching probability. Since it is not a volunteer service, we don't have any challenge in matching. We have a 30-minute free discussion and decide whether we are a match. After the free session, we sometimes refer them to other coaches who can support them better.
- There were 40 people who applied. We filtered them and only gave mentoring to 13 people.
- I also reject people who are in urgency and approach aggressively. If a person approaches us saying they have only one timeframe and need a job urgently, we also reject them.
- There are also some people who want to change careers — someone who is purely from the customer service field but wants to move to an IT career — it is difficult to support them.
- Mentees — what kind of information does the mentor need? We just need a CV, we don't ask for other specific requirements. The thirty-minute session decides if we are a match.
- We also provide direction to mid-career professionals, asking them what kind of work they are happy with, and give them a framework.
- Mentoring service — I get paid for it. In the long term, I am not sure when I will stop working. I enjoy mentoring and coaching — once I become stable, I'll keep doing the work I want to do; that's my motivation to do mentoring work.
- Scheduling is difficult — people working have different calendars and different Gmail accounts. The other side also shouldn't need to look at one's personal/informal information to be able to choose a time. Something like an AI secretary would help. It would be good if to-do lists and follow-up actions could be assigned on a calendar. At the office, Notion AI can be used, but Notion doesn't understand the Myanmar language.
